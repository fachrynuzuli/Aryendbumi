# The Digital Pathfinder: Fachry's Journey

In the sprawling industrial complex of Pangkalan Kerinci, where the humid Sumatran air carried the scent of paper production and diesel engines, Fachry Nuzuli Kamal stood watching a fleet of trucks roll into the yard. At 27, he was the youngest Project Lead in PT Prima Transportasi's history, orchestrating a digital transformation that few in the company truly understood.

"Another successful implementation," his colleague Budi remarked, nodding at the trucks now equipped with IoT sensors and predictive maintenance systems.

"Yeah, the system's fully operational now," Fachry replied, forcing enthusiasm into his voice. "Should reduce maintenance costs by at least 15% once we have six months of data."

"Good, good. The quarterly review is next week. Make sure those numbers are in the presentation," Budi said, already scrolling through his phone messages.

Fachry watched Budi walk away, noting how quickly the conversation had ended. No questions about how the system worked, what had made it successful, or what other possibilities it opened up. Just the numbers for the next PowerPoint slide.

That evening, in the company's spartan meeting room, Fachry presented the monthly progress update to the operations team. Rows of middle managers stared at him with expressions ranging from boredom to mild confusion.

"With the predictive maintenance system fully deployed, we can now anticipate component failures before they cause breakdowns," Fachry explained, clicking to a slide showing comparative data. "This chart shows—"

"How much is this saving us per quarter?" interrupted Pak Hendra, the Operations Director, not looking up from his laptop.

"Approximately 200 million rupiah per quarter once fully implemented," Fachry answered. "But the system also provides valuable data on driver behavior that could—"

"Good, good. Next agenda item," Pak Hendra said, still typing. "Procurement issues with the Dumai expansion."

Fachry sat down, his detailed slides about future possibilities untouched. On paper, he'd just shared a major win. In reality, no one had truly engaged with his work beyond the immediate cost-saving number.

## The Reality of the Corporate Ladder

Back in his cramped office – really more of a partitioned corner – Fachry reviewed his bank statement on his phone. Despite his title and responsibilities, his salary remained stubbornly mediocre. After taxes, loan payments for his engineering degree, and supporting his aging parents in Medan, there wasn't much left.

His phone buzzed with a notification from LinkedIn. A former university classmate, now at a prestigious multinational in Jakarta, had just been promoted to Senior Manager with a compensation package at least triple what Fachry was making.

"Everything okay?" asked Dhani, a junior analyst who shared the office space. "You look like you just got bad news."

Fachry quickly shut off his phone. "Just tired. Long day."

"Tell me about it. Hey, I wanted to ask you about that code you used for the driver fatigue monitoring. I was looking at the algorithm and—"

The office phone rang, interrupting them. Dhani picked up, listened briefly, then covered the receiver. "It's Procurement. Something about the invoice coding for the RFID system."

Fachry sighed and reached for the phone. Another administrative fire to put out, another distraction from the actual innovation work he'd been hired to do.

Later that night, in his modest one-bedroom apartment on the outskirts of town, Fachry opened his laptop to continue work on his side project – a digital course on prompt engineering for productivity. The apartment complex, built for mid-level employees, had thin walls. His neighbor's television blared a raucous game show, making concentration difficult.

"Creating Killer Prompts for Business Automation," he typed, then deleted it. "AI Prompt Engineering for Beginners," he tried instead, then deleted that too. The blank screen seemed to mock him.

His phone rang. It was his mother.

"Nak, how are you? Your father and I were wondering when you might come visit. It's been three months..."

"I'm good, Bu. Just busy with work. The new system launch took a lot of time."

"Is everything okay with your position? Your cousin Arman just got promoted at the bank in Medan. His parents are very proud."

Fachry closed his eyes, the familiar weight of expectations settling on his shoulders. "Everything's fine, Bu. The company values my work." The lie came easily after so much practice.

"Good, good. You know we always tell everyone how our son is a manager at a big company in Kerinci."

After the call, Fachry stared at his reflection in the darkened laptop screen. Project Lead. It sounded impressive. The reality was endless meetings, bureaucratic battles, and implementations that were watered down by budget constraints and resistance to change.

He opened a fresh document and began to type, not a course outline this time, but a journal entry:

*I'm trapped in a system that doesn't value what I can actually do. They want digital transformation but are afraid of actually transforming. They want innovation as long as it doesn't disrupt the hierarchy. I have the skills to build something meaningful, but I'm spending my days reconciling invoice codes and making PowerPoint slides for people who don't listen.*

*Meanwhile, my generation is split between those climbing a corporate ladder that leads nowhere and those dancing on TikTok making more in a month than I make in a year.*

The words flowed until past midnight, honest and raw. When he finally went to bed, the hollow feeling in his chest had expanded into an ache.

## Disconnection

The next morning, Fachry arrived early at the office to prepare for a stakeholder meeting about the new Driver Fatigue Control system. He'd worked weekends to perfect the presentation, researching global best practices and adding visualizations that even the most technology-averse executives could understand.

As meeting time approached, his phone buzzed with a message from Pak Hendra's assistant: "Meeting postponed to next week. Executives called to HQ for emergency budget review."

This was the third postponement. The system was already built and ready to deploy – a combination of wearable devices and machine learning algorithms that could prevent accidents by detecting fatigue patterns. Every week of delay meant greater risk to driver safety.

"Bad news?" asked Lisa, one of the few colleagues who seemed to genuinely understand Fachry's work. At 32, she was a veteran of the transport division who had embraced digitalization with unexpected enthusiasm.

"Postponed again," Fachry said, not hiding his frustration.

Lisa shook her head. "Same old story. Did I ever tell you about the inventory management system I proposed back in 2019? Six months of meetings, and they eventually implemented a watered-down version that saved maybe 10% of what it could have."

"How do you not get discouraged?" Fachry asked.

Lisa shrugged. "I focus on the small wins. And I keep a healthy life outside work." She paused. "Speaking of which, a few of us are going to that new café in town tonight. They have decent Wi-Fi. You should join."

Fachry almost declined automatically – his usual response to social invitations. But something in Lisa's understanding expression made him reconsider.

"Actually, that sounds good. I have some work I could do in a change of environment."

The café turned out to be one of those trendy spots trying to bring Jakarta vibes to small-town Kerinci – exposed brick, pendant lighting, and overpriced coffee. But the Wi-Fi was indeed decent, and the ambient noise was somehow less distracting than his apartment's thin walls.

"So what are you working on?" Lisa asked, glancing at Fachry's laptop. "Don't tell me it's the presentation they just postponed."

"No, this is... something else," Fachry admitted. "A side project."

Lisa's eyebrows rose. "You mean like consulting?"

"More like... education. I'm trying to build a course to teach people how to use AI tools effectively."

"Show me," she said, sliding her chair closer.

Reluctantly, Fachry turned his screen toward her, showing the half-finished course outline and sample videos. He'd expected polite disinterest or confusion, but Lisa's eyes widened.

"This is actually really good. Wait – you built that website yourself? Without a development team?"

"It's not that complicated. There are tools now that let you—"

"No, no, don't downplay it," Lisa interrupted. "Do you know how much the company paid that consulting firm for our intranet? And it's terrible. This looks better, and you're doing it alone?"

For the first time in months, Fachry felt a small glow of pride. "It's just a prototype."

"Have you shown anyone else this?"

"Not really. I mean, who would care? Most people here still think AI is science fiction."

Lisa looked at him thoughtfully. "You might be surprised. Not everyone is as stuck in their ways as the executives. There are others like me who know we need to adapt."

The conversation shifted to company gossip, but something had changed. Someone had seen his work – his real work – and understood its value.

## The Daily Grind

The momentary uplift from Lisa's interest faded quickly in the following days, as Fachry found himself buried in administrative tasks that had little to do with innovation. A procurement dispute had escalated, requiring him to compile three years of historical data. The HR department needed detailed job descriptions for the digital team's roles for a company-wide restructuring exercise that everyone knew would lead nowhere.

In a particularly soul-crushing meeting, Fachry sat silently as the Finance Director, Pak Joko, questioned every line item in the digital transformation budget.

"This software subscription – 15 million rupiah per month. Can't we just buy it once?"

"It's a cloud service," Fachry explained for the third time. "The subscription includes updates, security patches, and server costs."

"In my day, we bought software on a disk and it worked fine for years," Pak Joko insisted. "All these subscriptions are just ways for vendors to milk us."

"Sir, with respect, that approach is—"

"And these developer hours," Pak Joko continued, ignoring him. "Seems excessive. Can't we just hire a local college student? My nephew is studying computer science. He made a very nice website for his campus organization."

Fachry caught Lisa's eye across the table. She gave a small, sympathetic grimace.

Later, in the canteen, Fachry picked at his lunch while reviewing emails on his phone.

"Mind if I join you?" A tall man with a visitor badge stood by his table, tray in hand.

"Go ahead," Fachry replied automatically.

"I'm Arief, from the Jakarta office. Here for the systems integration assessment." He offered his hand.

Fachry introduced himself, expecting another bureaucratic conversation about implementation timelines or budget constraints.

"You're the one who developed the driver fatigue system, right? I read the proposal. The machine learning component is interesting. Did you train the model yourself?"

Surprised by the technical question, Fachry put down his phone. "Yes, actually. We collected data from 50 drivers over three months, combined with some pre-existing datasets on fatigue indicators."

The conversation quickly delved into technical details that Fachry rarely got to discuss at work. Arief asked insightful questions, challenged some assumptions, and offered suggestions from similar projects in Jakarta.

"Have you considered offering this as a product to other transportation companies?" Arief asked. "There's a huge market need, and what you've built is ahead of most commercial solutions."

"The company wouldn't be interested in becoming a technology provider," Fachry replied. "And anyway, I developed it on company time with company resources."

Arief shrugged. "True. But the concept, the approach – that's yours. And there's nothing stopping you from building something similar independently." He pulled out a business card. "I know some people in the startup ecosystem who would be interested in this kind of solution. If you ever want to explore options, give me a call."

Fachry took the card automatically, though he knew he would never use it. The thought of leaving the security of his position, mediocre as it was, for the uncertainty of a startup was terrifying. His parents would be devastated. His loan payments wouldn't wait for a business to become profitable.

Yet as he walked back to his office, the card seemed to burn in his pocket – a physical reminder that another path existed, even if he wasn't brave enough to take it.

## The Breaking Point

The quarterly leadership meeting should have been a triumph for Fachry. The initial data from his digital tire system showed a 5% extension in tire lifespan, translating to significant cost savings. The fleet management system was increasing utilization by precisely the 37 minutes per trip he had projected.

Instead, the presentation turned into an exercise in frustration.

"These are good incremental improvements," the CEO acknowledged after Fachry's presentation. "But we need to be thinking about more fundamental efficiencies in the current economic climate."

"Actually, sir," Fachry ventured, "if we integrate these systems and apply machine learning to the combined data, we could optimize routes dynamically based on real-time conditions. That would be a step-change improvement, not just incremental."

The CEO nodded absently. "Sounds interesting. Make sure to include that in next quarter's proposals. Now, moving on to the Dumai expansion challenges..."

Pak Hendra caught Fachry after the meeting. "Good work with the tire system. But maybe dial back the technical talk in these meetings. The board gets uncomfortable with things they don't understand."

"But sir, if they understood the potential—"

"They understand profit margins and risk, Fachry. That's their job. Ours is to deliver predictable improvements without rocking the boat." He patted Fachry's shoulder. "Keep it up with the solid implementations. Your annual review is coming up – could be a good outcome for you."

The promise of a modest raise after a year of innovation being systematically watered down was the final straw. That night, Fachry didn't even pretend to work on his side project. Instead, he drafted a resignation letter, pouring all his frustration into it.

*...while I appreciate the opportunities PT Prima Transportasi has provided, the company's resistance to meaningful digital transformation makes it impossible for me to deliver the value I know is possible...*

At 2 AM, his finger hovered over the send button. One click, and he would be unemployed, with no concrete alternative. His parents' voices echoed in his head: *"Job security is everything. Your generation doesn't understand how quickly things can fall apart."*

He closed the email draft without sending it.

Instead, he opened a new document and began writing – not a resignation, but a manifesto addressed to no one in particular:

*We were told to climb a ladder that no longer leads where we want to go. The Millennials who advised us are already at the top of systems that are slowly becoming irrelevant. The Gen Z who confuse us have never even acknowledged those systems. We are stuck between worlds, playing a game we can neither win nor quit.*

*My generation – born between 1992 and 2002 – was promised that education and corporate advancement would lead to security and prosperity. But the corporate world we entered doesn't value innovation; it fears it. It doesn't reward technical skill; it rewards conformity. Meanwhile, teenagers are building million-dollar businesses on platforms we barely understand.*

*We're trapped in systems designed by people who didn't grow up with technology but learned to use it, leading organizations that still treat digital as a department rather than a dimension. And we lack the audacity of those who never knew a world without smartphones and social media.*

The words flowed until dawn, raw and unfiltered. When he finally finished, exhaustion overtook him, and he fell asleep at his desk.

## Small Changes, Ripple Effects
